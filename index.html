  • People who suffer from chronic stress, or a kind of stress that is prolonged and affects day-to-day living, may turn to substance abuse or other addictive behaviors in order to cope.
  • They may intake stimulating substances in order to feel pleasure such as nicotine to dampen the stress that they are experiencing, or they may intake blunting substances such as alcohol to completely block out the effects of stress on their lives.
  • Most people who suffer from substance abuse often report a stressful event or experience prior to their addiction.
Chapter 2 THE TUG OF WAR

People are more likely to develop an addiction if a drug or a behavior is paired with a rewarding experience.



A gambling addict is rewarded when they score a big win and will repeat the behavior to experience that sensation of winning again.



An alcoholic may reinforce their behavior when they pair alcohol to pleasant situations such as parties, social-gathering, flirtatious interactions, etc.





Addiction can be further reinforced by the negative effects of withdrawal, or the unpleasant effects that come with stopping a behavior or an absence of a substance in the body.



⬤ Addiction alters the physical characteristics and functioning of the brain.

⬤ The reason why people become addicted to something has mainly to do with pleasure produced by these behaviors.

⬤ These pleasurable experiences are directly linked to the inner workings of the brain, particularly with neurotransmitters.

⬤ Neurotransmitters are like messengers, they carry signals or messages from one part of the brain to the other.

⬤ Drugs alter what kind of neurotransmitter is sent and the quantity sent. One particular neurotransmitter most commonly associated with addiction is dopamine.

⬤ Dopamine is a neurotransmitter that plays a role in motivation and pleasurable rewards.

⬤ It alters connections in the brain that makes it easier to repeat a behaviour over and over again.

⬤ This leads to habits and may end up as an addiction.

Chapter 3 EMERGING INTO LIGHT

Recovery from addiction is like navigating a tough journey, and having a supportive social network is like having a reliable map and companions along the way.

Picture it as a team cheering you on through the ups and downs. These are the folks who, knowingly or unknowingly, bring out the best in you.

Think of your support system as a diverse cast of characters, each playing a unique role.

They're the friends who inspire you with their healthy choices, the family members who stand by you without judgment, and the support groups where everyone understands the struggles because they've been there too.

It's like finding your squad - people who genuinely care about your well-being.

In the world of recovery, positive social influence is your secret weapon. Imagine it as a contagious positivity that helps reshape your habits. When you're surrounded by folks making good choices, it rubs off on you.

And the beauty is, you're not alone. You're part of a community where shared victories are celebrated and setbacks are faced together.

Recovery isn't a solo mission; it's a team effort. Your support system is there to keep you grounded, encourage every step forward, and remind you that resilience is not about going it alone.

It's about having your own personal fan club, ready to applaud your triumphs and help you back up when you stumble. So, if you're on the recovery path, know that having these supporters is like having a safety net

they make the journey not just bearable but conquerable.
